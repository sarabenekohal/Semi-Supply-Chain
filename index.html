How to read this book

This is a textbook, not a stock pitch. Its purpose is to give you the durable mental model of the AI infrastructure industry, so that any piece of news, earnings call, or product launch can be slotted into place. Specific tickers will rise and fall. The underlying physics, chemistry, and choke points will not.

You don't need any background in physics, computers, or finance. AI feels like software — a chatbot, an app, something that lives "in the cloud." But underneath every AI answer is a staggering amount of physical stuff: ultra-pure sand, billion-dollar machines, buildings the size of shopping malls, and enough electricity to power a city. The companies that own the hard-to-copy steps in this physical chain are quietly some of the most valuable businesses on Earth.

Every chapter follows the same four-question framework. Once you internalize it, you can apply it to any new company, technology, or layer of the stack you ever encounter:

Question 01
What does this step actually do?

Its physical or logical job, stripped of jargon. If you can't explain it in two sentences, you don't understand it yet.

Question 02
Who are the players?

The companies that own this step — ranked by structural position, not today's market cap.

Question 03
What is the moat?

Why a new entrant can't show up tomorrow and take this business. Scored from weak to strong.

Question 04
What could break it?

The emerging technology, geopolitical event, or business-model shift that could erode the moat.

Throughout, you'll see a moat-strength scale that rates how defensible each position is:

Fortress — a true monopoly or near-monopoly with no credible challenger in sight.
Strong — dominant share with high switching costs, but plausible challengers exist.
Contested — several credible players; the advantage rests on execution.
Commodity — low differentiation; profits compete down to the cost of capital.

The Mental Model

The Investor's Lens: moats, choke points & picks-and-shovels

Before we touch a single grain of sand, install three ideas in your head. Everything else in this book hangs off these three hooks.

1. The "picks and shovels" rule

During the 1849 Gold Rush, most people digging for gold went broke. The people who got reliably rich were the ones selling picks, shovels, jeans, and tents to every miner — no matter who struck gold. In AI, the "gold" is the winning chatbot or model, and that race is brutal and uncertain. The "picks and shovels" are the chips, machines, memory, cooling, and power that every AI company must buy no matter who wins. This book is mostly a tour of the picks-and-shovels business.

2. A moat is "why can't someone else just do this?"

A moat is a durable reason a company keeps its profits instead of competitors copying it and driving prices to zero. Five moats show up again and again in this industry:

The five moats you will see on every page

Keep these five icons in mind. For every company in this book, ask which of these it owns — and whether that moat is widening or shrinking.

3. Follow the choke points

A supply chain is only as fast as its narrowest step. The place where everyone is forced through one door is a choke point — and choke points are where pricing power, fat profit margins, and national-security drama all concentrate. Much of this book is a hunt for choke points: the single Dutch company that makes the machine that prints the chips, the single island that makes the best chips, the single connector that the data center can't run without.

📈 The Investor's Lens

The whole game in one sentence: find the steps that are physically hard, where one player owns the know-how, scale, or resource — and where demand is exploding regardless of which AI app wins. Now let's walk the chain and label every door.

I

From Sand to Chip

The physical journey: dirt → pure crystal → designed blueprint → printed chip → packaged brain → fed by memory.

Chapter 1

The Whole Map: from a grain of sand to a chatbot

Building and running an AI model is a physical act. Somewhere on Earth, sand is melted, refined to a purity human language strains to describe, and patterned by ultraviolet light into structures a few atoms wide. Those structures are stacked into chips, wired together with copper and glass, bathed in liquid coolant, and fed continuous electricity. The industry that does all of this is one continuous chain — and to understand any link, you have to see the whole.

So let's put the entire stack on one page before we zoom in. Don't worry about understanding every layer yet — each one gets its own chapter. The goal right now is to feel the shape of the chain and notice how few companies stand at each gate.

Figure 1.1

The AI infrastructure stack, end to end

★ Gold rows = highest pricing power. Lithography, compute, and power capture most of the industry's profit; the other layers are essential but earn more commodity-like margins. The chain runs both ways: a 2-year ASML delivery slip ripples up the stack; a grid shortfall ripples down it.

Three things to notice about this stack before we go deeper:

  • The chain is only as fast as its slowest link. In 2023 the bottleneck was wafer production. By 2024 it had moved to advanced packaging. By 2025, to electricity. The location of the bottleneck moves; the existence of one is permanent. (Chapter 15 traces this rotation in detail.)
  • Value capture is wildly uneven. Three layers — lithography, compute, and power — capture most of the industry's economic rent. The others are necessary, sometimes critical, but operate on thinner margins. Knowing where the rent pools is half of investment analysis.
  • Geographic concentration is extreme. One company in the Netherlands makes every cutting-edge lithography machine on Earth. One company in Taiwan manufactures most leading-edge logic chips. A few firms in South Korea and the US make nearly all advanced AI memory. Every shock to this geography matters.

The same journey, told as a story

Imagine following one shipment from start to finish:

  1. Raw Earth. A special quartz sand is purified until it is 99.999999999% pure silicon — cleaner than almost anything else humans make. Rare gases like helium and exotic metals are gathered too.
  2. Blueprint. Engineers use design software to lay out billions of microscopic switches, like drawing a city map with billions of streets, before anything is built.
  3. Printing. A machine the price of a jumbo jet uses precise light to "print" that map onto the silicon, layer by layer. This is the famous chip fab (factory).
  4. Packaging. The freshly printed brain is glued together with stacks of memory on a tiny shared floor, so they can talk at blistering speed.
  5. The Brain. Now you have a finished AI chip — a GPU, TPU, or custom ASIC (Chapter 7 explains the difference).
  6. Wiring. Thousands of these chips must be connected together with copper and beams of light so they act like one giant brain.
  7. Cooling. All that thinking creates ferocious heat. Liquid is pumped right onto the chips to carry it away.
  8. Power. The whole building drinks as much electricity as a small city — so power, not real estate, now decides where AI gets built.
  9. The Answer. Finally the AI model runs on this hardware and replies to you. The chain is complete.
🧒 Plain English

Think of it like a restaurant. Stages 1–4 are the farm and the kitchen prep. Stage 5 is the chef. Stages 6–8 are the dining room, plumbing, and electricity. Stage 9 is the meal arriving at your table. You only ever see the meal — but the money is made all the way down.

Why this map is the whole point

Notice something as you scan Figure 1.1: at almost every stage, the same two or three names appear. One company makes the printing machine. One island makes most of the best chips. One company's software locks in nearly every AI developer. These repeating names are the choke points, and spotting them is the entire skill this book is teaching you.

A long, complicated supply chain is not a weakness to an investor — it's a treasure map. Every link is a toll booth. Chapter 2

Raw Materials & Chemistry: the dirt that thinks

Every AI chip starts as a rock. Turning that rock into a flawless crystal — and gathering the strange gases and metals that go with it — is a chemistry problem so extreme that a handful of suppliers and a couple of countries control the whole base of the pyramid.

From beach sand to a perfect crystal

Chips are made from silicon, the second most common element in the Earth's crust. We have endless sand. So why is this a choke point? Because chips don't need sand — they need silicon so pure that, out of 100 billion atoms, only one is allowed to be something else. Reaching that purity is a multi-step chemical marathon.

Figure 2.1

Sand → wafer: five steps to a flawless silicon disc

A 300mm wafer is the round, mirror-shiny disc that hundreds of chips are printed onto at once. Companies like Shin-Etsu and SUMCO (Japan) dominate making these blank discs.

🍞
Analogy: Making polysilicon is like turning ordinary flour into a single, perfect, defect-free crystal of sugar the size of a person — and being told that one stray speck of pepper anywhere inside ruins the whole thing. The technique, not the ingredient, is the moat.

The specialty gases nobody talks about

Printing a chip isn't just light on silicon — it's a sequence of chemical baths, etches, and rinses using gases so specialized that running out of one can halt a $20-billion factory. Three matter most:

Helium (He)
Keeps lasers aligned and cools the back of wafers. It can't be manufactured — it's captured as a tiny byproduct of natural-gas drilling, and it literally leaks through solid steel over time, so you can't stockpile it. A textbook fragile-supply story.
Nitrogen trifluoride (NF₃)
The "oven cleaner" of chipmaking — it scrubs the inside of the machines between steps without eating the machine itself.
Tungsten hexafluoride (WF₆)
Used to lay down ultra-thin metal wiring that connects the transistor layers, like microscopic plumbing.
⛓️ Choke Point

Helium has no substitute and no synthetic source. A few industrial-gas giants — Linde (LIN), Air Liquide, Air Products (APD) — control gathering, purifying, and shipping these gases worldwide. They're boring, unglamorous, and quietly indispensable: the definition of a picks-and-shovels toll booth.

The rare metals — and the geopolitics baked into every chip

Beyond silicon, chips and data centers need a pantry of unusual metals. Here's where the story turns geopolitical: the mining and especially the refining of these materials is concentrated in just a few countries — and one country, China, dominates the refining of many of them. Refining is the real choke point, because building a new refinery is dirty, slow, and capital-intensive.

Notice the pattern: the resource moat here belongs less to whoever owns the dirt and more to whoever owns the refining. That's why "rare earth" headlines are really about processing capacity and export policy. For investors this is a double-edged sword — enormous pricing power, but exposed to government decisions overnight. Western efforts to build independent refining (e.g. MP Materials (MP) in the US, Lynas (LYC) in Australia) are a direct bet that this choke point is too dangerous to leave concentrated.

🚀 Emerging Disruption

Synthetic diamond substrates. As chips run hotter, the silicon itself becomes a thermal bottleneck — heat can't escape fast enough through copper. Synthetic diamond, grown by a process called chemical vapor deposition (CVD), conducts heat about five times better than copper, and could let chips run at roughly double the power density without overheating.

The two companies furthest along: Element Six, a UK-based subsidiary of De Beers (privately held, no ticker), is the clear leader — it launched a commercial copper-diamond composite called Cu-Diamond at Photonics West in January 2025, with thermal conductivity around 800 W/mK (roughly double copper's), and it partners with Japan's Orbray on wafer-scale single-crystal diamond. So this is past proof-of-concept and into early revenue, though still small versus traditional thermal materials. Coherent (COHR) — also covered in the interconnects chapter for optical components — participates through related thermal and optical applications rather than as a direct Cu-Diamond competitor.

The watch item is adoption inside leading-edge AI accelerator packages. If TSMC or a hyperscaler designs diamond heat-spreaders into a production AI chip, that's the inflection. As of writing it is being evaluated rather than designed-in at volume.

Chapter 3

Designing the Chip: the software that draws the brain

Before a single atom is printed, the entire chip is built and tested inside a computer. A modern AI chip has tens of billions of transistors — no human could place them by hand. The tools that do this, and the pre-made building blocks they snap together, are a quiet, brutal near-monopoly.

What is a transistor, really?

A transistor is just a microscopic on/off switch. That's it. String billions of them together in clever patterns and you can do math, store memories, and run an AI. A modern chip packs tens of billions of these switches into a fingernail-sized square — so designing one is like planning the road network of ten thousand cities, where every road is a few atoms wide and a single wrong turn ruins the chip.

🧒 Plain English

If a chip is a city of billions of streets, then EDA software (Electronic Design Automation) is the city-planning program, and IP blocks are pre-built neighborhoods you can drop in instead of designing every house yourself.

Figure 3.1

The design layer: two markets that gate every chip on Earth

Two separate toll booths sit in front of every chip: the tools you design with, and the instruction set (the chip's native language) you build on.

EDA: the three companies that gate everyone

You cannot design a competitive modern chip without software from essentially three companies: Synopsys (SNPS), Cadence (CDNS), and Siemens EDA. NVIDIA (NVDA), Apple (AAPL), Google (GOOGL), and every startup all pay them. Their moat is a beautiful flywheel: they work hand-in-glove with the factories (like TSMC), so their tools are certified for each new manufacturing process. No serious factory will print a design that wasn't verified in trusted tools — so trust itself becomes the moat.

IP: who owns the chip's "language"

Every chip understands a set of basic commands called an instruction set. Three matter:

  • ARM (ARM) — licensed into almost every smartphone and increasingly into data centers. Its moat is the ultimate network effect: millions of programs are already written for it.
  • x86 — owned by Intel (INTC) and AMD (AMD), the long-time king of PCs and servers.
  • RISC-V — a free, open standard anyone can use. It's the disruptor: no license fees, no single owner. Companies adopt it to escape paying the toll.
📈 The Investor's Lens

EDA is one of the best businesses in tech almost nobody outside the industry knows: tiny, irreplaceable, charges recurring license fees, grows every time chips get more complex. Classic know-how + lock-in moat. ARM is a network moat — its risk is RISC-V slowly chipping away at the free end. When evaluating any "design layer" company, ask: is its lock-in getting stronger as chips get harder, or is an open standard eroding it?

🚀 Emerging Disruption

AI designing AI chips. Synopsys and Cadence now use AI to lay out chips automatically, compressing months of human work into days. This widens their moat (their tools get smarter and harder to leave) while accelerating the whole industry — a rare case where the toll booth and the highway both speed up.

Chapter 4

Printing the Chip: the most extreme machine ever built

This is the beating heart of the whole supply chain — and home to its two most absolute monopolies. One Dutch company makes the only machine that can print the most advanced chips. One Taiwanese company runs that machine better than anyone. Understand this chapter and you understand why "the chip war" is a thing.

How you "print" something smaller than a virus

Chipmaking is basically the world's most precise photography. You shine light through a stencil (a "mask") onto the silicon wafer, which is coated in a light-sensitive film. Where the light hits, the pattern hardens; the rest washes away. Repeat this dozens of times, building the chip up in layers like a microscopic 3-D printer. The catch: the features are now so small that ordinary light is too "fat" to draw them.

🖍️
Analogy: Drawing today's chips with normal light is like trying to sign your name on a grain of rice using a fat marker. You need an impossibly fine pen. That pen is EUV light — and only one company on Earth makes the machine that wields it.

EUV: the machine that prints the future

Extreme Ultraviolet (EUV) lithography uses light with an incredibly tiny wavelength to draw the smallest features. Making this light is almost absurd: a machine fires a laser at droplets of molten tin 50,000 times a second, vaporizing them into plasma that glows in EUV, which is then focused by the flattest mirrors humans can make. Each machine costs as much as a commercial airliner and ships in dozens of crates.

Figure 4.1

Inside an EUV machine — simplified

Only ASML (Netherlands) makes EUV machines — a 100% monopoly. The next generation, High-NA EUV, prints even finer and costs roughly double.

⛓️ Choke Point

This is the single most concentrated choke point in the entire economy. ASML (ASML) has a literal 100% monopoly on EUV — the only path to the most advanced chips. It took 30 years and an entire ecosystem of suppliers (like Zeiss for the mirrors and Cymer for the light source, both now tightly tied to ASML) to build one. This is why export controls on "who is allowed to buy an ASML machine" are front-page geopolitics.

The foundry: fabless vs. integrated

Owning the machine isn't enough — you need a factory (a fab) and the secret recipes to run it at high yield (the share of chips that come out working, not defective). Two business models split the industry:

  • Fabless + foundry. A "fabless" designer (NVIDIA, Apple, AMD) designs the chip but owns no factory. They hand the blueprint to a foundry — a for-hire factory — to print it. The dominant foundry is TSMC (TSM) (Taiwan).
  • Integrated (IDM). One company designs and builds, like Intel (INTC) and Samsung (Korea-listed). Intel is now also trying to become a for-hire foundry.

The "nanometer" ladder — and why it matters

You'll hear chips described as "5nm," "3nm," "2nm." Smaller numbers = newer generation = more switches packed in, running faster and using less power. (The number is now more of a marketing label than a literal measurement, but smaller still means newer.) Climbing this ladder costs exponentially more each step, which is why fewer and fewer companies can afford to stay at the front.

Figure 4.2

The leading-edge node ladder — and how the pack thins out

As nodes shrink, the cost to compete explodes and the field collapses to one or two players. At the very front, it's effectively just TSMC, with Samsung and Intel chasing.

📈 The Investor's Lens

This chapter contains two of the deepest moats in the world: ASML (know-how, an unrepeatable 30-year monopoly) and TSMC (scale + know-how, the best yields on the planet plus customer trust). The flip side is concentration risk: most leading-edge chips are made on one island in a geopolitically tense region. That single fact drives hundreds of billions in government subsidies (the US CHIPS Act, Japan, Europe) trying to build fabs elsewhere — a multi-decade investment theme in itself.

🚀 Emerging Disruption

High-NA EUV. ASML's next machine prints even finer features and is the gateway to 2nm-and-below. Whoever masters it first (TSMC vs. Intel vs. Samsung) could leap ahead — making High-NA adoption one of the most-watched horse races in tech.

Chapter 5

Advanced Packaging: gluing the brain to its memory

For decades, the chip was the product. Now the hottest bottleneck in all of AI isn't printing the chip — it's assembling the finished chip, its memory, and its connections onto one tiny shared floor. This step, called advanced packaging, is the reason AI chips are sold out years in advance.

Why packaging suddenly matters

An AI chip has two halves: the logic (the part that thinks) and the memory (the part that holds the data it's thinking about). They need to talk to each other constantly and at insane speed. If they're far apart, the chip spends all its time waiting — like a chef who has to run to a different building every time they need an ingredient. Advanced packaging puts them millimeters apart on a shared base so they can talk almost instantly.

Figure 5.1

Cross-section of a modern AI accelerator package (e.g. TSMC CoWoS)

Logic in the middle, towers of HBM memory on the sides, all sitting on a silicon interposer — the brain ends up just millimeters from its memory, so almost no time is wasted fetching data. TSMC's version of this floor is called CoWoS; Intel's competing approach is EMIB.

Chiplets: building chips like LEGO

Chips have gotten so big and expensive that making one giant piece is risky — one defect ruins the whole thing. The fix: build several smaller pieces (chiplets) and snap them together in the package, like LEGO bricks. You mix-and-match the best pieces, improve yield, and upgrade one brick without redesigning everything. Nearly every leading AI chip is now a collection of chiplets pretending to be one chip.

🧒 Plain English

HBM = "High-Bandwidth Memory" = memory chips stacked into a tall tower so a huge amount of data can flow at once. Interposer = the super-fast floor everything sits on. CoWoS = TSMC's brand name for building that floor.

⛓️ Choke Point

Here's the kicker: the company that makes the best chips (TSMC) also dominates the best advanced packaging (CoWoS). So the same company is the bottleneck twice. For years, the limiting factor on how many top AI chips the world can build hasn't been the chip — it's been packaging capacity. When you read "AI chips are supply-constrained," this is usually why.

📈 The Investor's Lens

Packaging turned a sleepy back-end step into a front-line moat. Watch three things: (1) TSMC's CoWoS capacity expansions — they gate the entire GPU supply; (2) OSAT firms (outsourced assembly & test) like ASE Group (ASX) and Amkor (AMKR) that handle packaging for others; (3) equipment makers whose tools do the stacking and bonding (e.g. BE Semiconductor / Besi (BESI), Applied Materials (AMAT), Lam Research (LRCX)). When a single step gates the whole industry, the suppliers to that step are a leveraged bet on it.

🚀 Emerging Disruption

Glass substrates & panel-level packaging. The next leap is replacing the base material with glass, which stays flatter and lets even more chiplets pack together — and building packages on big rectangular panels instead of round wafers to cut cost. Intel, Samsung, and glassmakers like Corning (GLW) are racing here. (More on glass connections in Chapter 9.)

Chapter 6

Memory & the DRAM Cycle: the most dramatic business in tech

Memory is where AI keeps the data it's actively thinking about. It's also the most boom-and-bust business in the entire chain — periods of fat profits followed by brutal crashes, over and over. Understanding why it's so cyclical, and why AI may finally be breaking the cycle, is a core investor skill.

Memory vs. logic: a desk and a filing cabinet

If the chip's logic is the brain doing the thinking, memory is the desk it works on. There are two main kinds:

  • DRAM — fast "working memory." This is the desk: whatever you're actively using sits here. It forgets everything when power is cut. HBM (Chapter 5's memory towers) is a premium, stacked version of DRAM built for AI.
  • NAND flash — slower "storage." This is the filing cabinet: it holds data long-term, even when powered off (it's what's inside your phone and SSDs).
🗂️
Analogy: DRAM is your desktop — fast to reach, but cleared every night. NAND is the filing cabinet — slower, but it keeps your files forever. AI needs a gigantic desk (HBM), which is exactly the part that's hardest to make.

The big three — and why they all do the same thing at once

Almost all the world's advanced memory comes from just three companies: Samsung and SK Hynix (both Korea-listed) and Micron (MU) (US). With so few players making a near-identical product, memory behaves like a commodity — like oil or wheat. And commodities are cyclical.

Figure 6.1

The memory cycle — why prices boom and bust on repeat

When prices are high, all three rush to build factories — which takes ~2 years. They all arrive at once, flood the market, and prices crash. Then they cut back, supply tightens, and the cycle restarts.

Why it's cyclical (the trap, step by step)

  1. Demand rises, memory gets scarce, prices spike, profits soar.
  2. All three makers decide to build new factories to grab the profits.
  3. Factories take ~2 years and billions to build — so supply can't respond quickly.
  4. Two years later, all the new capacity switches on at the same time → sudden glut.
  5. With identical products and too much supply, prices collapse below cost.
  6. Makers cut output and freeze spending → eventually shortage returns → back to step 1.
📈 The Investor's Lens

This is the classic commodity cyclical trap: no lock-in, no differentiation, so profits get competed away on a repeating wave. The investor's job is to buy near the bottom (when everyone's losing money and cutting capacity) and sell into the boom — the opposite of what feels comfortable. The danger is mistaking a cyclical peak for a permanent new normal.

How AI is bending the cycle: HBM

Here's the twist that makes memory exciting right now. HBM — the stacked memory AI chips crave — is much harder to make than ordinary DRAM. Yields are lower, it uses far more wafers per gigabyte, and it's sold on long contracts to a few giant customers. That makes HBM look less like a pure commodity and more like a specialty product with pricing power — and it soaks up so much manufacturing capacity that it tightens the ordinary DRAM market too.

Custom HBM & "memory near compute." II

The Chips Themselves

Now we have a finished brain. But there are different kinds of brain — built for different jobs, by different companies, through different supply chains.

Chapter 7

GPU vs TPU vs Trainium: the three roads to an AI chip

"AI chip" isn't one thing. There's the general-purpose workhorse anyone can buy (the GPU), and there are custom chips that tech giants design for themselves (TPU, Trainium). They do similar math but travel completely different supply chains — and represent two opposing investment bets.

First, what is an AI chip actually doing?

Almost all AI boils down to one operation done trillions of times: multiplying big grids of numbers (called matrix math). The whole game is doing this specific math as fast and as cheaply as possible. Every chip below is just a different strategy for that one task.

🧒 Plain English

GPU = a flexible chip you can rent or buy from NVIDIA, good at everything parallel. ASIC ("application-specific") = a chip custom-built for one job, so it's cheaper and more efficient at that job but useless at others. TPU and Trainium are ASICs that big tech companies designed for their own AI.

Figure 7.1

Three chips, three supply chains

Notice the punchline: all three roads pass through the same two villages — TSMC (making/packaging) and the big-three HBM makers (memory). The chip designs differ; the deep supply-chain choke points are shared.

What's actually inside an AI GPU?

A CPU (the chip in your laptop) does a few tasks very fast, one after another — a sprinter. A GPU does many tasks at once, each slightly slower — a marathon of ten thousand runners moving together. For matrix math, that parallel approach wins overwhelmingly. But here's the surprise when you open the package: an AI GPU is mostly memory.

Figure 7.2

A GPU's anatomy at a glance

The logic die in the center does the math, but the speed at which it can be fed data from the surrounding HBM stacks is what actually determines training performance. This is why HBM supply (Chapter 6) is as strategic as GPU supply.

The merchant chip: NVIDIA's GPU

NVIDIA (NVDA) doesn't keep its chips — it sells them to everyone: startups, governments, cloud companies. Its real moat isn't only the silicon; it's CUDA, the software developers have used for 15+ years. Millions of AI programs are written for CUDA, and rewriting them for a rival chip is painful — so even when a competitor's hardware is good, customers stay. (Chapter 12 digs into this.)

The custom chips: TPU & Trainium

Google (GOOGL) (TPU) and Amazon (AMZN) (Trainium/Inferentia) buy huge numbers of NVIDIA GPUs — but they also design their own chips to cut their dependence and their costs. They don't sell these chips; they rent them to you through their clouds. They lean on Broadcom (AVGO) (and Marvell (MRVL)) for the hard networking pieces and on TSMC to build them. Microsoft (MSFT) (Maia) and Meta (META) (MTIA) are doing the same.

📈 The Investor's Lens

This is the central debate in AI hardware investing: merchant (NVIDIA) vs. custom (ASIC). NVIDIA wins on flexibility, software lock-in, and being available to all. Custom chips win on cost and efficiency for the giant who designed them. The quiet winner either way is Broadcom, which helps build most of the custom chips — and, of course, TSMC, which builds all of them. When two armies fight, sell ammunition to both.

⛓️ Choke Point

No matter which chip "wins," the same handful of suppliers gets paid: TSMC (fab + packaging), the big-three memory makers (HBM), and ASML (the machines behind it all). This is why those names are so valuable — they're indifferent to the war happening above them.

Chapter 8

Training vs Inference: two completely different jobs

An AI model has two phases in its life, and they need almost opposite hardware. Training is teaching the model (done once, enormously expensive). Inference is using the model (done billions of times, must be cheap and fast). New companies like Groq and Cerebras exist precisely because these two jobs reward different chip designs.

🎓
Analogy: Training is like sending someone to medical school for years — slow, brutally expensive, done once. Inference is that doctor then seeing patients all day — it has to be fast, cheap, and repeatable millions of times. You'd staff those two jobs very differently.

Figure 8.1

Training vs Inference at a glance

Training is a one-time mega-project; inference is a forever-cost. As AI gets used more, the world's spending tilts steadily from training toward inference.

Why training needs a "single giant brain"

Training a frontier model is too big for one chip, so thousands are wired together to act as one. The hard part isn't the chips — it's keeping them all in sync. If one chip waits on another, the whole cluster stalls. That's why training hardware obsesses over interconnects (Chapter 9) and huge memory. NVIDIA dominates here because its chips and its networking and its software all work together out of the box.

Why inference is the bigger long-term prize

You train a model once, but then it answers questions forever. Over a model's life, the electricity and chips spent answering dwarf the cost of training. So inference is where efficiency — answers per watt, answers per dollar — wins. This is the opening that lets specialist startups challenge NVIDIA.

The realistic bull case for challengers is owning the high-volume, cost-sensitive inference slice — not dethroning NVIDIA in training.

🚀 Emerging Disruption

Inference moving to the edge. As models shrink, more inference will run directly on phones, laptops, and cars instead of in data centers — pulling in Apple (AAPL), Qualcomm (QCOM), and a wave of low-power "NPU" chips. The center of gravity in AI compute may slowly spread out from giant data centers toward billions of small devices.

Chapter 9

Interconnects: copper, light & glass

A modern AI cluster is tens of thousands of chips that must behave like one giant brain. The wiring between them — the interconnect — is now as important as the chips. And there's a quiet war over what that wiring is made of: copper, beams of light through glass fiber, or a new idea of putting light right next to the chip.

Why the wiring is the whole game

If chips can't pass data to each other fast enough, adding more chips is pointless — they just sit waiting. As models grow, more and more of a cluster's cost and energy goes into moving data, not computing it. The company that moves data fastest, cheapest, and coolest has enormous leverage.

🧒 Plain English

Copper = electricity through metal wires (cheap, simple, but fades over distance). Optical / fiber = data sent as flashes of light through glass threads (fast over distance, but needs lasers to convert electricity↔light). Co-packaged optics = putting those lasers right next to the chip so signals turn into light immediately.

Figure 9.1

Copper vs Optical vs Co-Packaged — pick your trade-off

Rule of thumb: copper for the shortest hops (inside a rack), optics for distance (between racks/buildings). The frontier is pushing optics ever closer to the chip itself.

The unsung hero: the retimer

As copper signals travel even a short distance at high speed, they get garbled. A retimer is a tiny chip that cleans up and re-sends the signal so it arrives crisp. It sounds mundane, but in a dense AI server there are many of them, and they're getting more essential as speeds climb. Astera Labs (ALAB) built a strong business here, with a sticky twist: its monitoring software (COSMOS) gets baked into the motherboard, creating lock-in beyond the chip itself.

The networking layer: NVLink, InfiniBand, Ethernet

Above the physical wires sits the "language" the chips use to talk:

  • NVLink (NVIDIA (NVDA)) — ultra-fast links between NVIDIA chips sitting close together. Proprietary, and a big part of NVIDIA's moat.
  • InfiniBand (NVIDIA, via its Mellanox acquisition) — connects whole clusters; long favored for training.
  • Ethernet — the open standard the whole internet uses. A broad alliance (Broadcom (AVGO), Arista (ANET), others) is pushing "AI Ethernet" as the open challenger to NVIDIA's proprietary networking.
⛓️ Choke Point

NVIDIA doesn't just sell chips — it increasingly sells the entire networked system (chips + NVLink + InfiniBand + software). By owning the wiring and the brains, it makes the whole cluster a package only it can fully optimize. The open-Ethernet camp (Broadcom, Arista) is the main force trying to pry that open.

📈 The Investor's Lens

Interconnect is a rare spot with several distinct ways to invest: retimers (ALAB Astera), optical components (LITE Lumentum, COHR Coherent), networking silicon & switches (AVGO Broadcom, ANET Arista), and connectors/cables (APH Amphenol). As clusters scale, the share of the budget spent moving data grows — making "the plumbing between chips" one of the most durable secular growth themes in the build-out.

🚀 Emerging Disruption

Co-packaged optics & "optical I/O." The big leap is converting electricity to light right at the chip's edge, slashing the power wasted shoving electrons down copper. Startups like Ayar Labs and giants like NVIDIA, Broadcom, and TSMC are racing here. And further out, new hollow-core / specialty glass fibers (where Corning plays) could carry light faster and with less loss — the "glass" front of the copper-vs-light war.

III

The Building

The chips are made and wired. Now they need a home that can cool them, power them, and run software to orchestrate them — the data center.

Chapter 10

The Data Center: a building that drinks heat

An AI data center is less like an office and more like a power station crossed with a plumbing factory. Cramming thousands of red-hot chips into one room creates two giant engineering problems: getting the heat out and getting the power in. Whole industries exist to solve just those two problems.

The heat problem

A single modern AI server rack can draw ~100 kilowatts or more — roughly the power of 50–70 homes, packed into one cabinet. All that electricity turns into heat. Old-school air cooling (fans blowing cold air) simply can't keep up anymore. So the industry has moved to liquid cooling, because liquid carries heat far better than air.

Figure 10.1

Three ways to cool an AI rack — increasing intensity

As chips run hotter, the industry slides down this scale from air → cold plate → immersion. Each step removes more heat but adds plumbing complexity.

The plumbing nobody sees

Liquid cooling means real plumbing inside a building full of electricity — a scary combination. Key pieces:

CDU
"Cooling Distribution Unit" — the pump-and-control box that circulates coolant in a sealed loop to the racks.
Cold plates
Metal plates that sit directly on each chip; coolant flows through them to grab the heat.
Quick-disconnects
Special leak-proof couplings so technicians can swap servers without spilling fluid near live power. Patents here are a real moat.
Busways
Thick copper overhead tracks that carry power cleanly across the room to each rack.
🧒 Plain English

Immersion cooling sounds insane — dunking computers in liquid — but the fluid is special: it doesn't conduct electricity, so the chips work fine while bathed in it. The fluid boils on the hot chip, the vapor rises and cools, and drips back down — cooling with no fans at all.

Who builds this

The "data-center plumbing and power gear" companies have quietly become AI winners:

  • Vertiv (VRT) — cooling and power systems, a marquee liquid-cooling beneficiary.
  • Modine (MOD), nVent (NVT), plus private specialists like Boyd and LiquidStack — heat exchangers, cold plates, immersion.
  • Eaton (ETN), Schneider Electric, ABB — power distribution, transformers, switchgear.
  • Super Micro (SMCI), Dell (DELL), HPE (HPE) — the server boxes themselves.
📈 The Investor's Lens

This is the most "boring" part of the chain and one of the most leveraged to AI. These firms sell more content per rack every year as cooling and power needs intensify — a rising tide regardless of which chip wins. The moats are real but smaller (patents, engineering, installed-base relationships) than the silicon monopolies, so watch competition and margins. Still: when you can't tell which AI app will win, "sell the cooling and power to all of them" is a clean thesis.

🚀 Emerging Disruption

Two-phase immersion & direct-to-chip at scale. The next step removes pumps and manifolds almost entirely by letting fluid boil on the silicon (two-phase). Companies like Vertiv and LiquidStack are commercializing it. If it works at scale, it could reset the economics of how densely chips can be packed.

Chapter 11

Energy & the Grid: the real ceiling on AI

Here's the plot twist of the whole industry: the thing most likely to slow AI down isn't chips — it's electricity. The chips exist; the money exists; what's scarce is enough reliable power, in the right place, soon enough. AI's growth has run head-first into the slow, creaky physical power grid.

Why power became the bottleneck

A big AI campus can need hundreds of megawatts — even a gigawatt — of continuous power. That's the output of a serious power plant, running 24/7. The catch: getting permission to plug a new mega-load into the grid involves multi-year studies by grid operators (with names like PJM, ERCOT, MISO). This "interconnection queue" can take 3–5 years. AI moves in months; the grid moves in years.

🚰
Analogy: It's like buying the world's most powerful shower but discovering the city's water main can't deliver enough water — and the permit to upgrade the pipe takes five years. The bottleneck isn't the shower; it's the pipe.

Figure 11.1

Powering a data center — your options, by speed and reliability

The dream square is top-left: reliable and fast. Nothing sits there cleanly — which is why operators stack several options and why power has become a strategic moat in itself.

The workarounds — and the companies behind them

  • On-site generation. Don't wait for the grid — build your own power next to the data center. Natural-gas turbines and engines (e.g. GE Vernova (GEV), Caterpillar (CAT)) can be deployed relatively fast.
  • Fuel cells. Bloom Energy (BE) makes box-like cells that turn natural gas or hydrogen into electricity without burning it, deployable in ~a year — skipping the grid queue entirely.
  • Nuclear (the long game). Tech giants are signing deals to restart or build reactors. Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) from Oklo (OKLO) and NuScale (SMR) aim to drop factory-built reactors right next to a campus for steady, carbon-free power.
  • The grid itself. Utilities and independent power producers like Vistra (VST), Constellation (CEG), and NRG (NRG) suddenly have the scarcest asset of all: existing, connected, reliable generation.
  • The "arms dealers" of power. Eaton (ETN), Schneider Electric, GEV, and Quanta Services (PWR) sell the transformers, switchgear, and grid build-out everyone needs.
⛓️ Choke Point

A site with a pre-approved grid connection — or its own power source — is now worth a fortune, because the connection is the scarce thing, not the land. This has flipped the data-center business: power availability, not real estate price, decides where AI gets built. It's also why electricity-equipment makers and power producers re-rated as "AI stocks."

Stranded power, flared gas & the bitcoin-miner pivot

If power is the bottleneck, then wasted power is treasure. Two related stories follow from that.

Crusoe (private) built its business on stranded energy — power that exists but has nowhere to go. At remote oil wells, natural gas comes up as a byproduct and is often flared (burned off uselessly) because there's no pipeline. Crusoe parks modular data centers right at the wellhead, burns that otherwise-wasted gas to generate electricity, and turns it into compute. Same idea at remote solar/wind sites that can't get their power to a city. It's a clever way to get electricity today without joining the multi-year grid queue, and Crusoe has since expanded into building large AI data centers more broadly.

The bitcoin-miner pivot. Bitcoin miners spent a decade doing one thing brilliantly: securing huge blocks of cheap power and energizing buildings full of hot chips. That is exactly the scarce skill set AI now needs. So many miners are converting (or leasing) their sites into AI data centers — names you'll hear include Core Scientific (CORZ), TeraWulf (WULF), IREN (IREN), Cipher Mining (CIFR), Riot Platforms (RIOT), Hut 8 (HUT), and Applied Digital (APLD). Some signed multi-billion-dollar deals to host hyperscalers and AI clouds.

📈 The Investor's Lens — telling the legit from the lottery ticket

A bitcoin miner's stock jumping on an "AI pivot" press release is not the same as a real business. Five questions separate substance from story:

  1. Real, contracted power? Do they actually hold a large, energized grid connection (hundreds of MW) with room to grow — or just a plan? Energized megawatts are the asset.
  2. A signed, creditworthy tenant. Is there a long-term lease with a real hyperscaler/AI cloud (investment-grade counterparty), or just a non-binding "letter of intent"? The contract length and customer quality are everything.
  3. Can the site actually host AI? Bitcoin sites are often air-cooled and remote. AI needs liquid cooling, high-grade networking, redundancy, and low-latency fiber. Retrofitting is expensive and slow — does the capex math work?
  4. Balance sheet for the build. AI data centers cost far more per megawatt than mining rigs. Can they finance the conversion without diluting shareholders into oblivion?
  5. Mix shift in the numbers. Is high-margin, recurring AI-hosting revenue actually showing up in results — or is it still 95% volatile mining revenue with an AI logo on the slides?

Rule of thumb: the legitimate ones are really power-and-real-estate companies (selling energized, AI-ready buildings on long leases); the lottery tickets are mining companies hoping a press release re-rates the stock. Judge them on contracted megawatts and tenant quality, not headlines.

📈 The Investor's Lens

Energy is the second-order AI trade. The chip names are crowded; the power names are a way to bet on AI's growth from a different angle — utilities with spare baseload, electrical-equipment makers, gas-turbine builders, and the nuclear optionality plays. Caveats: utilities are regulated and slow, fuel-cell and SMR names can be richly valued on future promise, and demand forecasts swing wildly. Separate "sells real power today" from "promises power in 2030."

🚀 Emerging Disruption

Behind-the-meter nuclear & AI-flexible demand. Two frontiers: (1) reactors built specifically to power one AI campus, bypassing the grid; and (2) making data centers flexible — dialing compute up and down to match when power is cheap and available, turning AI clusters into giant adjustable loads that actually help stabilize the grid.

Chapter 12

The Software Stack: the invisible layers that run it all

We've built the chips and the building. But hardware is useless without software at every level — from the code that talks to the chip, to the system that schedules thousands of them, to the AI model itself. Software is where some of the strongest and stickiest moats in the entire industry live, including NVIDIA's most important one.

The stack, from metal to chatbot

Think of software as a layer cake sitting on top of the hardware. Each layer hides the messy details of the one below so the layer above doesn't have to care.

Figure 12.1

The AI software stack — and who owns each layer

Each layer is a potential business and a potential moat. The gold layer — the software that actually talks to the chip — is where NVIDIA built the lock-in that protects everything above it.

CUDA: the moat that makes NVIDIA un-copyable

NVIDIA's competitors can build a fast chip. What they struggle to copy is CUDA — the software toolkit NVIDIA has refined for over 15 years that lets developers actually use the chip easily. Nearly every AI researcher learned on CUDA; nearly every AI tool is built for it. Switching to a rival means rewriting and re-testing mountains of code. That's lock-in in its purest form: the hardware advantage may be temporary, but the software habit is sticky for years.

🧒 Plain English

Imagine everyone in the world learned to type on one keyboard layout. A "better" keyboard might exist, but nobody wants to relearn typing. CUDA is that keyboard layout for AI. AMD's ROCm is the rival layout trying to win people over.

The other software layers worth knowing

  • Frameworks — PyTorch (originally Meta, now broadly governed) and JAX (Google) are how researchers describe models. PyTorch winning the open framework is a big reason AI moved so fast.
  • Serving / inference engines — tools like vLLM and NVIDIA's TensorRT squeeze more answers per chip. Pure efficiency, pure money.
  • Orchestration — software (often built on Kubernetes) that schedules thousands of chips, restarts failed jobs, and keeps clusters busy. Boring, essential, sticky.
  • The clouds — where it all runs: hyperscalers (Microsoft (MSFT) Azure, Amazon (AMZN) AWS, Google (GOOGL) Cloud, Oracle (ORCL)) and "neoclouds" built only for AI (next section).
  • The models — OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, Meta (META), Mistral, xAI — the brains that everything above is built to deliver.

Neoclouds: the specialist tier

A new kind of cloud has appeared that does one thing: rent out AI compute. These "neoclouds" differ from the hyperscalers in three structural ways:

  • Pure-play. They don't sell databases, storage, or general computing — just GPU hours, sometimes with a thin software layer on top.
  • Higher utilization. Without the overhead of a full cloud, they keep GPUs busier, which means lower prices for customers.
  • Tighter NVIDIA relationships. Most buy almost exclusively from NVIDIA and often get preferential chip allocation in exchange for big early purchase commitments.

The LLMs: tokenization, partnerships & the economics

At the very top of the stack sit the Large Language Models (LLMs) — the "brains" like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini. They're the reason the entire chain below them is being built. Here's how they actually work and make money, in plain English.

What is a "token"? (the unit AI is sold in)

An LLM doesn't read words — it reads tokens, which are word-fragments. "Tokenization" is just chopping text into these pieces (roughly ¾ of a word each, so ~750 words ≈ 1,000 tokens). The model's whole job is to predict the next token, over and over, to produce an answer. Tokens are also the unit of money: AI companies charge per million tokens in (your prompt) and per million tokens out (the answer). Everything about AI economics — cost, price, profit — is measured in tokens.

🚕
Analogy: Think of an LLM like a taxi that bills by the word. Tokens are the meter. "Training" is the cost of building the car and teaching the driver (huge, one-time); each ride you take ("inference") burns fuel (electricity + chip time) and is billed by the token.

How LLM economics work

  • Training cost — a giant one-time bill (chips, power, data, salaries) to create the model. Can run into the hundreds of millions or more for a frontier model.
  • Inference cost — what it costs to answer each query: chip time + electricity, measured per token. This is a forever cost and, at scale, dwarfs training.
  • Revenue — subscriptions (e.g. $20/month chatbots), per-token API fees to developers, and enterprise deals.
  • The squeeze — token prices have been falling fast (competition + efficiency), while the cost to train the next, smarter model keeps rising. So the core question for any model company is: can it earn back rising training bills before falling prices erode the margin?

Who partners with whom (and why)

Models need oceans of compute, so each leading lab is tied to a cloud/chip backer:

  • OpenAI — historically anchored to Microsoft (MSFT) Azure, and increasingly multi-cloud (Oracle, CoreWeave, and its own data-center efforts).
  • Anthropic — backed by Amazon (AMZN) and Google (GOOGL); uses AWS Trainium and Google TPUs as well as NVIDIA.
  • Google DeepMind — runs on Google's own TPUs (the most vertically integrated of all).
  • Meta (META) — trains open-weight Llama models on massive in-house NVIDIA fleets and its own MTIA chips.
  • xAI — built huge NVIDIA GPU clusters at speed.
📈 The Investor's Lens

Notice the circular flow of money: hyperscalers invest in model labs → labs spend that money renting the hyperscalers' compute → hyperscalers buy chips from NVIDIA → NVIDIA's revenue soars. It's real demand, but it means a lot of AI revenue currently circulates among a handful of giants. Watch for that loop to be validated by outside revenue (real customers paying for AI), not just partners funding each other.

The deeper question: does value flow to the hardware or the software, and how durable is each moat? NVIDIA proves software can protect hardware (CUDA). But the model layer faces fierce competition and may behave more like a commodity than people expect — with durable profits sitting in distribution (the clouds and apps with real users) and in the picks-and-shovels below. When you analyze any AI company, locate it on this cake and ask what stops the layer above or below from capturing its margin.

🚀 Emerging Disruption

Cracking the CUDA moat. Open compilers and "write once, run anywhere" projects (e.g. Triton, OpenAI's and others' efforts) aim to let code run on any chip, not just NVIDIA's. If they succeed, the hardware playing field levels and competition shifts back to raw price-performance — the single biggest swing factor for the whole industry's profit map.

Frontier & Frameworks

Where the industry is heading next — and the mental rulers (scaling laws, the Pareto frontier) investors use to measure whether the whole bet is working.

Chapter 13

The Frontier: the wild bets that could rewrite the chain

Every chapter so far had its own "emerging disruption." This chapter gathers the biggest, most speculative leaps — the moonshots that, if they work, could reorder the entire supply chain. Treat these as optionality: low odds individually, but enormous payoffs, and exactly where "where does the frontier go next?" thinking earns its keep.

Frontier 1

🛰️ Orbital data centers

Put data centers in space. Two real advantages: endless solar power (no night, no clouds in the right orbit) and free cooling by radiating heat into the cold of space — no water, no chillers.

The catch: cosmic radiation flips bits and damages chips (needs special radiation-hardened silicon), and moving data up/down needs precise laser links.
Players: SpaceX (launch), and a wave of satellite/optical-link firms.

Frontier 2

🧠 Neuromorphic & analog in-memory

Today's chips waste huge energy shuttling data between memory and logic. In-memory computing does the math inside the memory itself, potentially cutting inference power dramatically.

The catch: analog computing is imprecise and hard to program.
Players: startups like Rain, Syntiant, d-Matrix; research arms of the giants.

Frontier 3

💡 Photonic & optical computing

Compute with light instead of electricity for parts of the math. Light is fast and runs cool — a natural fit for the matrix math AI lives on.

The catch: light is great at some operations, awkward at others; hybrids are most likely.
Players: Lightmatter, Celestial AI, plus the co-packaged-optics crowd from Chapter 9.

Frontier 4

⚛️ Quantum (the long shot)

A fundamentally different kind of computer that could crack specific problems classical chips can't. Not a near-term AI-training replacement — more a parallel track.

The catch: still early, error-prone, and narrow.
Players: IBM (IBM), Google (GOOGL), IonQ (IONQ), Rigetti (RGTI), PsiQuantum (private).

📈 The Investor's Lens

Frontier bets follow a barbell logic: most fail or arrive late, a few pay off spectacularly. Two practical filters: (1) does it ride an existing supply chain or need a brand-new one? (riding existing rails — like co-packaged optics through TSMC — is far more investable than inventing a new industry); and (2) is it solving the binding constraint — power and heat — rather than a problem we've already mostly solved? The frontier tech aimed at energy efficiency and cooling has the clearest path to mattering.

⛓️ Choke Point

Even the moonshots lean on the same gatekeepers: orbital and photonic chips still get built by TSMC and need ASML's machines; in-memory chips still need advanced packaging and HBM-like memory. The frontier rarely escapes the choke points — it usually depends on them. That's a recurring reason the deepest-moat names keep winning across regime changes.

Chapter 14

Scaling Laws & the Pareto Frontier: how we measure the bet

Why are companies spending hundreds of billions on chips? Because of a startling discovery: AI gets predictably smarter when you feed it more computing power. That pattern — the scaling laws — is the engine of the entire boom. This chapter explains the rulers used to measure AI progress, and where we sit on them today.

The scaling laws: the most important chart in tech

Around 2020, researchers found that an AI model's "smartness" improves in a smooth, predictable way as you increase three things together: more computing power, more data, and a bigger model. Crucially, the improvement is reliable enough to plan around — spend 10× more compute and you get a measurable, expected step up in capability. That predictability is what justified the gigantic spending: it turned "AI might get better" into "AI will get better if we buy more chips."

🧒 Plain English

It's like discovering that a plant grows taller in a steady, measurable way every time you double its sunlight. Once you trust that rule, it makes sense to build a giant greenhouse. The "sunlight" here is computing power, and the chips are the greenhouse.

Figure 14.1

A scaling law — more compute, predictably lower error (a straight line on a "zoomed" log scale)

The magic is the straightness: progress is predictable. The open question (red) is whether the line keeps going or starts to bend as we run low on data and easy gains.

Where are we on the curve? The three live debates

  1. Are we running out of data? We've nearly used the readable internet. The frontier is shifting to synthetic data (AI making its own training data) and higher-quality curation.
  2. Is "training scaling" slowing — and is "thinking time" the new axis? A major shift is test-time / inference-time compute: instead of only making models bigger, let them "think longer" when answering hard questions. This opens a second scaling law — and it makes inference compute (Chapter 8) far more valuable.
  3. Does it ever stop? Nobody knows. Bulls see a clear runway to far more capable systems; skeptics expect diminishing returns. Trillions of dollars of investment ride on this single uncertainty.
📏 The Rulers: how AI compute is measured

FLOPs — "floating-point operations": the raw count of math steps. Training a frontier model takes an almost unimaginable number of them. More FLOPs ≈ more capability (per the scaling laws).

Performance-per-watt — capability per unit of electricity. Since power is the bottleneck (Chapter 11), this is arguably the most important efficiency metric of all.

Performance-per-dollar — capability per unit of money. This is the metric that decides whether a challenger chip can beat NVIDIA in practice.

Tokens-per-second & latency — how fast a model produces words, and how long you wait. The key inference-quality metrics.

Utilization (e.g. "MFU") — what fraction of a chip's theoretical power you actually use. Real clusters often waste a lot; closing that gap is pure profit.

The Pareto frontier: the "no free lunch" map

Almost every choice in this industry is a trade-off: faster usually costs more power; cheaper usually means slower. The Pareto frontier is the line of best possible trade-offs — the options where you can't improve one thing without sacrificing another. Anything on the line is a smart, efficient choice; anything below it is simply a bad deal (something else beats it on every count).

Figure 14.2

The Pareto frontier — best trade-offs vs. "dominated" (bad) options

When NVIDIA, Groq, Cerebras, or a TPU is compared, this is the real picture: each occupies a spot on (or below) the frontier of performance vs. cost vs. power. A new chip only matters if it pushes the green line outward.

📈 The Investor's Lens

These frameworks turn hype into judgment. (1) Scaling laws are the demand thesis — if they hold, compute demand keeps compounding and the picks-and-shovels keep selling out; if they break, the whole capex wave is at risk. (2) The Pareto frontier is the competition thesis — a rival chip is only investable if it genuinely moves the frontier, not if it's a "dominated" me-too. (3) The shift to inference-time compute reshuffles who wins: it tilts value toward efficient inference and the firms that serve it cheaply. Hold these three rulers up to every AI claim you hear.

🚀 Emerging Disruption

New scaling axes. Beyond bigger models, the frontier is exploring scaling reasoning time, scaling the number of AI "agents" working together, and scaling data quality over quantity. Each new axis is a fresh source of compute demand — and a reason the build-out may run longer than a single scaling law would suggest.

Cross-layer dynamics: how the bottlenecks talk to each other

The single most important habit to develop is to stop thinking of each layer as independent. They are not. A constraint in one layer reshapes pricing, capital spending, and competitive position several layers away. When the bottleneck moves, the money moves with it.

🚿
Picture the stack from Figure 1.1 as a water pipe with sections of different width. Flow is set by the narrowest section. Widen it and flow doesn't rise smoothly — it surges until it hits the next narrowest section, which becomes the new bottleneck. Pricing power, returns, and investor attention all migrate to wherever the pipe is currently narrowest.

The history of AI infrastructure since 2022 is a clean illustration:

  • 2022–2023 — the wafer bottleneck. TSMC's leading-edge wafer output was the binding constraint; NVIDIA couldn't get enough. Pricing power and returns concentrated in TSMC (TSM) and NVIDIA (NVDA).
  • 2024 — the packaging bottleneck. Wafers loosened, but CoWoS advanced-packaging capacity didn't keep up. TSMC's CoWoS plans became the most-watched line in the industry; packaging-adjacent names (Besi, the OSATs) re-rated.
  • 2024–2025 — the HBM bottleneck. Packaging expanded, but HBM memory couldn't keep pace; SK Hynix sold out 12–18 months ahead. The constraint rotated to memory, and HBM makers' equity reflected it.
  • 2025 onward — the power bottleneck. HBM expanded; the binding constraint left the silicon stack entirely and moved into the grid. Constellation (CEG), Vistra (VST), GE Vernova (GEV), Eaton (ETN), and Bloom Energy (BE) became the new beneficiaries.

None of this was random — each constraint was foreseeable from the capacity curves of the previous one. The investor's job is to be one bottleneck ahead.

Worked example: what if memory stops being a bottleneck?

Suppose the next HBM generation ramps faster than expected, supply catches up, and HBM prices soften. Tracing the ripples shows how cross-layer thinking works:

Three cross-layer relationships worth memorizing

  1. Power ↔ chip pricing. When power (not chips) is the hard ceiling, the price of any one chip matters less than its performance-per-watt — buyers will pay almost anything for the chip that delivers the most tokens per watt. This structurally supports NVIDIA's pricing power as long as it leads on that metric, and it's the deepest reason hyperscalers tolerate NVIDIA's margins. Watch performance-per-watt as closely as raw performance.
  2. Cooling intensity ↔ rack economics. When cooling is the bottleneck, customers pay up for high-density liquid-cooled racks, pulling value into Vertiv (VRT), Schneider, nVent, and Modine. But if a future chip generation delivers far more compute per watt, the cooling capex per dollar of GPU spend falls — cooling vendors still grow, just slower. Their structural risk is power density staying extreme.
  3. Software lock-in ↔ ASIC volume. CUDA's strength is exactly what keeps hyperscaler ASICs (TPU, Trainium, Maia) volume-limited rather than capability-limited. If CUDA's grip weakens (ROCm maturing, open Ethernet, hardware-agnostic PyTorch), ASIC volumes rise faster — pulling value toward Broadcom (AVGO), Marvell (MRVL), and Astera, and away from NVIDIA's pricing power. This is the single largest cross-layer wager any AI investor implicitly makes.

The TSMC "Goldilocks" problem

One framing — articulated well by the investor Gavin Baker — ties the whole stack together through a single company's discipline: TSMC is acting as a "governor" on the AI boom by deliberately refusing to expand capacity as fast as customers like NVIDIA want.

Management remembers past memory and foundry cycles where capacity raced ahead of demand and crashed pricing for years. So they ramp deliberately — sometimes painfully so. The striking implication: if TSMC doubled capacity, NVIDIA could likely sell vastly more chips next year. But TSMC won't double. And because it won't, the AI build-out stretches across more years instead of climaxing in one over-supplied crash.

Why this is structural, not a quirk

It's the rational behavior of an oligopolist with very long-lived, expensive assets. A leading-edge fab takes 3–5 years and $20–30 billion. Over-build and demand normalizes → you sit on depreciating capacity earning commodity returns for a decade. Under-build → you keep premium pricing and customers absorb the discomfort. The asymmetry strongly favors caution. The same instinct makes ASML expand deliberately and power producers refuse to sign every contract on the table. Capital-disciplined oligopolists at the choke points are why this cycle has lasted longer without breaking than a naïve "build-out" model predicts.

Too Slow
Demand frustrated

If capex stays well below need for years, AI gets bottlenecked, hyperscalers lose patience and accelerate their own chips. Pricing stays firm but volume growth slows. Picks-and-shovels revenue decelerates even with healthy margins.

Just Right
The likely zone

TSMC adds capacity each year fast enough to keep utilization high (mid-90s%) and let customers grow — but never fully satisfies them. NVIDIA stays supply-constrained, margins across the stack stay high, and the build-out extends multi-year instead of crashing.

Too Fast
The bubble trigger

If TSMC accelerates hard — political pressure to onshore, or to grab share — wafer and CoWoS supply could catch up within 12–18 months. Pricing softens, NVIDIA margins compress, growth slows just as new capacity hits depreciation. The classic semiconductor cycle ending.

Outside Triggers
Demand shocks

The other failure mode is demand-side: AI workloads smaller than expected, a model-capability plateau, or a recession cutting hyperscaler capex. In any of these, even a disciplined ramp looks "too fast" because demand fell out from under it.

Four scenarios worth stress-testing

Not predictions — scenarios for pressure-testing any position you hold.

Scenario A
The Taiwan Shock

A blockade, conflict, or accident disrupts TSMC. Within months, leading-edge supply collapses globally; every layer above silicon is constrained. Taiwan-exposed names hit hardest; US-manufacturing names (Intel Foundry, Micron) gain temporary share but can't replace lost capacity for years.

Scenario B
The Power Wall

Grid interconnection delays drag on. The build-out slows even as chip supply normalizes. Behind-the-meter generation (Bloom, gas turbines, eventually SMRs) becomes strategic. Players holding existing power positions gain a structural edge.

Scenario C
The Software Switch

ROCm maturity + open Ethernet + hyperscaler frameworks erode CUDA's grip. NVIDIA's hardware stays excellent but margins compress as customers gain real second-source leverage. ASIC and AMD share grows; picks-and-shovels (Broadcom, Astera, packaging, HBM) are unaffected.

Scenario D
Continuation (most likely)

Constraints get worked through one at a time. Bottlenecks rotate chips → packaging → HBM → power and back. The industry keeps growing because demand is real and players are well-capitalized. Tickers rise and fall on execution; the structural moats largely hold.
